Guide
Getting Started
1
Power on the drone

Press the LiteBee Wing power button. Wait for the status light to stop flashing and settle to a slow blink (ready mode).

2
Join the drone WiFi

Open System Settings → Wi-Fi and connect to LiteBee Wing_XXXXXXXX. Your internet will drop — this is normal. The drone network is 192.168.10.x.

3
Click Connect

The app automatically finds the right network interface, pings the drone, and completes the UDP handshake. You have up to 8 seconds — the dot will blink yellow while it tries.

4
Watch telemetry appear

Once the Drone dot turns green, live battery %, altitude, and orientation data will populate within one second. The drone is now ready for commands from the webapp.

Status Indicators
Green

Connected and healthy. WebSocket server is up; drone is responding to keep-alive pings.

Yellow (blinking)

In progress. The app is actively trying to connect — scanning interfaces, pinging the drone, or retrying the handshake.

Red

Error or disconnected. Check the Diagnostics log below for the specific error code and resolution hint.

Grey

Idle / not yet attempted.

NIC
Interface row

Shows which network adapter was selected (e.g. en0 192.168.10.2). Re-picked fresh on every Connect — joining the drone WiFi after launch is fine.

Telemetry Reference
Battery %

Remaining charge. Turns yellow below 30 %, red below 15 %. Land before it hits 10 %.

Altitude (cm)

Height above the takeoff surface, measured by the downward rangefinder. Resets to 0 on each takeoff.

Yaw (°)

Horizontal rotation from the heading at power-on. −180 to +180°. Positive = clockwise.

Pitch / Roll (°)

Forward/back and side tilt angle. Near zero when hovering level. Large values indicate active movement or wind.

Vx / Vy

Forward and lateral velocity from the IMU (cm/s). Both near zero means the drone is hovering in place.

Update rate

Capped at 10 Hz in this app (drone sends up to 100 Hz). Values show the last received packet.

Controls
CON
Connect

Runs the full connection sequence: interface pick → gateway ping → UDP handshake → telemetry listener → keep-alive. Must be connected to the drone WiFi first.

DIS
Disconnect

Cleanly closes all UDP sockets and stops the keep-alive timer. The drone will auto-land after ~15 s without keep-alive signals.

TST
Self-Test

Runs a 5-step diagnostic: lists interfaces, pings the drone, measures handshake latency, queries battery, and counts telemetry packets. Use this first when troubleshooting. Results stream into the Diagnostics log and can be copied for support.

Error Codes
NO_INTERFACE

No network adapter found in the drone subnet (192.168.10.x or 192.168.43.x). Join the drone WiFi and click Connect again.

DRONE_UNREACHABLE

Interface found but the drone gateway didn't respond to ping. Power-cycle the drone and wait for its light to settle before retrying.

HANDSHAKE_TIMEOUT

Three command → ok attempts all timed out. Usually a firewall blocking outbound UDP to port 8889. Check Security settings on macOS or Windows Defender rules.

HANDSHAKE_SLOW

Informational — still trying on attempt 2 or 3. Not a failure yet; wait for the outcome.

SOCKET_BUSY

Port 8890 (telemetry) is already in use. Close any OEM drone apps, OBS, or other tools that listen on UDP 8890 and reconnect.

FIREWALL_BLOCKED

The OS denied the UDP socket. On macOS: System Settings → Privacy & Security → Firewall → allow this app. On macOS 14+: grant Local Network permission when prompted.

DRONE_LOST

Keep-alive pings stopped getting replies for 15 s after a successful connection. Drone likely flew out of WiFi range or powered off. Click Connect to re-establish.

BAD_RANGE

A set_position command from the webapp was outside the drone's legal range (−500 to +500 cm). Check the flight program in the webapp.

Stuck? Click Self-Test before filing a support ticket — it runs all five diagnostic checks and gives you a copy-pasteable report that covers most common issues in one go.
